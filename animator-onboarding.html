Case Study · Learning Experience Design

Onboarding the next generation of
Vietnamese animators into the Japanese pipeline.

A self-guided, scenario-based training program built in a visual novel engine — designed to standardize production at Sino Studio, an indie animation studio with no formal training infrastructure.

Role Learning Experience Designer · Sole IDer
Client Sino Studio (Vietnam)
Tools Ren'Py · Canva · Clip Studio Paint
Audience Vietnamese animators, ages 16–30
01 / Context

A studio building anime without a shared vocabulary.

Sino Studio chose to follow the Japanese animation pipeline, but most of its in-house animators had only ever encountered it through word-of-mouth and untranslated source material.

Vietnam has no formal animation school operating at industry level. As a result, Sino Studio's process became an unstable hybrid — Western terminology stitched together with fragments of Japanese workflow, learned informally and inconsistently across the team.

The studio director, while experienced as an independent animator, did not come from a formal industry background. Many parts of the workflow ran on instinct rather than a defined pipeline. There was no standard, no onboarding, and no shared language.

The result was a cascade of recurring production problems: improper animation techniques, incorrect line weight, inaccurate colors, and constant rework — driving up cost, slowing delivery, and making it harder to collaborate with international freelancers who used different terms for the same things.

↳ 01

No formal training infrastructure or dedicated trainer.

↳ 02

Pipeline terminology fragmented across English, Japanese, and Vietnamese.

↳ 03

Zero allocated budget for tooling or external course platforms.

02 / Approach

A self-paced training that looks and feels like the work itself.

I designed a structured onboarding module delivered as an interactive visual novel — fully virtual, reusable, branching by role, and built on a free engine (Ren'Py).

Animators choose their own learning path: a complete beginner can walk through the full Japanese pipeline before touching Sino-specific protocols, while a senior hire can skip directly to studio standards. A standalone "Review by Topic" path lets existing employees revisit a single stage when they need to.

The training simulates the actual Clip Studio Paint work environment — including cut-folder briefs, canvas-setup dialogs, layer specs, and timesheet annotation — so that practicing the workflow inside the training is indistinguishable from doing the job.

↳ THE CORE INSIGHT

If the audience already plays games, teach them through a game.

The trainees are 16–30, predominantly male, tech-savvy, and culturally fluent in anime/manga and visual novels. A VN-style format wasn't a gimmick — it was the lowest-friction interface this audience could possibly receive training through. Branching dialogue replaced static slides; in-engine simulations replaced video walkthroughs; built-in save/load replaced an LMS.

03 / Method

From learner analysis to a working build, end to end.

I owned this project end-to-end: research, SME interviews, instructional design, UX, illustration, scripting in Ren'Py, and beta evaluation. The process below traces the journey from the first action map to the deployed build.

i. Action Map: Anchoring the training to four observable actions.

Before writing a single line of content, I mapped the training to specific, observable behaviors animators would need to perform on the job. This kept the design honest — every lesson had to trace back to an action the learner would actually take in production.

▸ Objective: Standardize Sino Studio's production output to meet industrial Japanese standards.
  • ↳ Identify the roles of LO, Genga, and Douga and the unique delivery requirements for each.
  • ↳ Apply studio-specific environmental settings in Clip Studio Paint to ensure asset portability.
  • ↳ Apply mandatory color-trace lines (Red/Blue/Green) to Genga layers for the Shiage process.
  • ↳ Read and write annotations on the timesheet and animation frames.

Working with an external SME — a Vietnamese animator with deep experience in the Japanese industry — I cross-checked every objective against real production requirements and reconciled bilingual terminology before any of it touched the storyboard.

ii. Text Storyboard: Scripting the conversation between trainer and learner.

Because the format is a visual novel, the storyboard is essentially a screenplay. I drafted every screen as a text card — scene description, trainer dialogue, and learner choice points — before any visuals were made. This let me iterate cheaply and validate the pedagogical flow before committing art assets.

Each "scene" includes formative checks: a knowledge or scenario question with immediate feedback. Learners cannot proceed past a wrong answer until they reselect — but feedback is framed as teaching, never punishment.

iii. Visual Mockups: A four-color system, designed for focus.

I designed the UI in Canva, restricting the palette to four colors checked for contrast. Minimalism wasn't aesthetic preference — it was a constraint: this training will eventually run inside the studio's main website, so the build had to be light in memory and free of visual noise.

I designed the trainer mascot (the studio director's persona) with multiple poses and emotions to give the lessons rhythm and personality, and used a license-free icon pack from Irasutoya for consistency throughout the lessons.

iv. Interactive Prototype: Branching dialogue, choice boxes, and a flipbook viewer.

The first interactive build wired up the core mechanics: branching dialogue, choice-based formative assessment, and a custom flipbook component that toggles between still-frame inspection and GIF-style playback — letting learners study an animation cycle at their own pace.

Every formative check loops until the learner reaches the correct answer — but the feedback explains why, so each attempt is genuinely instructive rather than a guessing game.

v. Full Development: A working simulation of the Clip Studio Paint environment.

The summative assessment is a high-fidelity simulation: learners receive a "cut folder," set up their canvas, select correctly-spec'd reference images, apply color-trace lines, and submit for review — all inside a clickable replica of Clip Studio Paint built from layered images and Ren'Py button logic.

An auto-updating to-do checklist tracks progress. The first run is guided by the trainer; the second run is solo and graded. An 80% threshold is required to earn the certificate of completion.

Beyond the lessons, I built out the surrounding system to feel like a real product: a save/load system that persists across sessions without an account, an in-context terminology dictionary covering Japanese / English / Vietnamese, and full audio with adjustable BGM and SFX in a settings menu.

04 / Outcome

A reusable onboarding system, built for zero budget.

The final product is a fully self-paced, branching training module that any new hire at Sino Studio can complete independently — with a built-in certificate gate before they touch live production work.

Evaluation follows Levels 1 and 4 of the Kirkpatrick model. Beta testers from across the target audience rate the experience and flag confusing terminology; post-implementation, I'll compare production mistakes from existing employees before and three months after rollout, and survey editors and compositors on changes in their rework load.

↳ What's next

If post-implementation data shows reduced rework and improved efficiency, the studio director has indicated approval for additional internal projects to formalize Sino Studio's broader workflows. If not, the data tells us where to look.
